## Day 10 -- Ofsted Explainers

**Post type:** Carousel (4 slides)

**Caption:**

How often does Ofsted actually inspect nurseries? And what triggers an early visit?

Here's what most parents don't know:

Routine inspections happen roughly every 4 years for nurseries rated "Good" or "Outstanding." That means a report could be quite old and the nursery might have changed significantly.

Nurseries rated "Requires Improvement" get re-inspected within 12 months.

"Inadequate" nurseries get re-inspected within 6 months and may face enforcement action in the meantime.

Early inspections can be triggered by:
- Parent complaints to Ofsted
- Safeguarding concerns
- A significant change in leadership or ownership
- Information from other agencies

This is why we show the inspection date clearly on every nursery profile. If a report is more than 4 years old, we flag it so you know to ask the nursery what's changed since then.

"I should send my child to nursery full-time so they don't miss out on learning."

Actually, that's not what the research says.

For children under 3, the evidence suggests that 2-3 days per week is a sweet spot. It gives them enough time to build relationships with staff and other children, follow routines, and benefit from structured activities -- without being overwhelming.

Quality always beats quantity. A child who attends three days at a wonderful nursery will thrive more than one who attends five days somewhere mediocre.

Some children do brilliantly full-time. Others need more time at home. There's no single right answer -- it depends on your child's temperament, your family setup, and your work commitments.

The best arrangement is the one that works for your whole family without burning anyone out.

Tax-Free Childcare is one of the most underused benefits in the UK. Here's exactly how it works.

You pay into a government childcare account. For every 8 pounds you put in, the government adds 2 pounds. That's a 25% top-up, up to 2,000 pounds per child per year (or 4,000 pounds for disabled children).

Who qualifies:
- You and your partner both need to be working
- Each earning at least the equivalent of 16 hours at minimum wage
- Neither earning over 100,000 pounds per year

How to use it:
1. Apply at childcarechoices.gov.uk
2. Set up your Tax-Free Childcare account
3. Pay your nursery directly from the account
4. The government tops up every payment automatically

You can use it alongside the 30 hours free entitlement. Yes, both. At the same time.

If you're not using this yet, you could be leaving thousands of pounds on the table.

**Post type:** Carousel (5 slides)

**Caption:**

How to read an Ofsted report like a pro (in 5 minutes flat):

Most parents just look at the overall rating. But the real gold is in the detail. Here's where to focus:

1. Skip to "What the setting needs to do to improve." This tells you exactly what Ofsted thinks is lacking. Even Outstanding nurseries have this section.

2. Read the "Personal development" section. This is about how children behave, build relationships, and develop independence. It matters hugely.

3. Check the "Leadership and management" paragraph. High staff turnover or weak leadership are red flags that affect everything else.

4. Look at the date. If it's more than 3 years old, the nursery may have changed significantly. Ask them about it.

5. Notice the language. "Occasionally" and "sometimes" are Ofsted code for "not enough." "Consistently" means they're doing it well.

Armed with this, you'll get more from a 5-minute read than most parents get from an hour-long visit.

## Day 18 -- Ofsted Explainers

**Post type:** Single image (infographic)

**Caption:**

What happens when a nursery gets rated "Requires Improvement"?

It's not as scary as it sounds. Here's the process:

First, Ofsted writes a detailed report explaining exactly what needs to change. The nursery has to create an action plan showing how they'll fix each issue.

Then, Ofsted returns within 12 months -- sometimes sooner -- to check progress. If the nursery has improved, it moves back to "Good." If not, it may drop to "Inadequate."

Here's the thing: many nurseries rated RI are already making changes by the time the report is published. Some improve dramatically within weeks.

Should you avoid RI nurseries entirely? Not necessarily. Read the report carefully. If the issues are about paperwork or planning rather than the quality of care your child receives, it might still be a great fit.

But do ask the nursery directly: "What have you changed since the inspection?" A good nursery will be open and honest about it.

## Day 20 -- Parent Q&A / Myth-Busting

**Post type:** Carousel (4 slides)

**Caption:**

"Nurseries are just glorified babysitting."

Nope. Not even close.

The Early Years Foundation Stage (EYFS) is a statutory framework that every nursery in England must follow. It covers seven areas of learning:

- Communication and language
- Physical development
- Personal, social, and emotional development
- Literacy
- Mathematics
- Understanding the world
- Expressive arts and design

Your child's key worker plans activities specifically designed to support development across all of these areas. They observe, assess, and track your child's progress. They identify where extra support might be needed.

Children who attend quality early years provision arrive at school more confident, more socially skilled, and more ready to learn. This is backed by decades of research.

So no, it's not babysitting. It's one of the most important educational experiences your child will ever have.

## Day 21 -- Nursery Tips & Checklists

**Post type:** Carousel (5 slides)

**Caption:**

Red flags to watch for at a nursery (that aren't always obvious):

1. Staff on their phones. Every setting has a mobile phone policy. If staff are scrolling during the session, ask about it.

2. Children wandering aimlessly. Good nurseries have a mix of free play and guided activities. If children look lost or bored, the provision might be thin.

3. A locked or unused outdoor area. Children should be going outside every day, in almost all weather. An immaculate garden is suspicious -- it should look well-used.

4. Vague answers to your questions. A confident nursery will answer openly about ratios, staff qualifications, and how they handle behaviour. Evasion is a red flag.

5. No evidence of children's work on display. Walls should show artwork, photos of activities, and learning journeys. A bare setting can suggest low engagement.

Trust what you see, not just what you're told.

## Day 26 -- Ofsted Explainers

**Post type:** Carousel (5 slides)

**Caption:**

5 things Ofsted inspectors actually look for (that might surprise you):

1. Interactions, not activities. They watch how staff talk to children, not just what activities are on offer. Are conversations back-and-forth? Are staff extending children's thinking?

2. Safeguarding culture. Not just the policy in the folder -- do ALL staff know what to do if they have a concern? Inspectors will ask individual staff members directly.

3. The curriculum intent. What is the nursery trying to achieve? Is there a clear plan for what children should learn and experience? Is it ambitious enough?

4. How well the nursery knows each child. Inspectors ask key workers about individual children's progress and next steps. Vague answers are a red flag.

5. Behaviour and attitudes. They observe how children behave when staff aren't directing them. Are children engaged, curious, and kind to each other?

Understanding what Ofsted values helps you ask better questions on your visits too.

## Day 28 -- Parent Q&A / Myth-Busting

**Post type:** Single image (text-based graphic)

**Caption:**

"My child doesn't need nursery. They get enough socialisation at playgroups."

Playgroups are brilliant. But they're not quite the same thing.

At playgroup, your child has you as their secure base. They explore from the safety of knowing you're right there. That's lovely, but it's a different skill to the independence they develop at nursery.

At nursery, children learn to:
- Build trust with other adults (not just family)
- Navigate conflict with peers without a parent stepping in
- Follow a routine set by someone else
- Self-regulate emotions in a group setting
- Wait, take turns, and negotiate

These are skills that prepare children incredibly well for school. Research from the EPPE study showed that children who attended quality pre-school settings had better social and academic outcomes at age 11 -- and the benefits lasted into secondary school.

Playgroups and nursery aren't either/or. Many families do both, and that's a great combination.
