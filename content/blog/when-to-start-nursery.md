# When Should My Child Start Nursery? The Complete Guide

Deciding when your child should start nursery is one of those questions with no single right answer. Some families need childcare from six months, others wait until three or four. The right timing depends on your circumstances, your child, and the options available to you. This guide walks through the key considerations at each age, the signs of readiness to look for, and how to make the transition as smooth as possible.

## The Legal Position

In England, there is no legal requirement for children to attend nursery at any age. Compulsory education does not begin until the term after a child's fifth birthday. Nursery attendance is entirely a family choice.

However, the government-funded childcare entitlements are structured around specific ages, which naturally influences when many families start nursery:

- **From 9 months:** Eligible working parents can access 15 funded hours per week
- **From age 2:** Eligible working parents can access 30 funded hours per week
- **From age 3:** All children receive 15 hours universal entitlement; eligible working parents receive 30 hours

For full details on funded hours and eligibility, read our [free childcare hours guide](/blog/free-childcare-hours-guide).

## Starting Nursery at Different Ages

### 6 to 12 Months

Babies can attend nursery from around six months, and some settings accept them from three months. At this age, babies need a high level of individual attention, consistent carers, and a calm, responsive environment. The legal staff ratio for under-twos is 1:3, meaning each carer looks after a maximum of three babies.

**Advantages:** Babies this young often adapt quickly because they have not yet developed strong separation anxiety (which typically peaks between 8 and 18 months). Starting early also allows you to return to work sooner if needed.

**Considerations:** Some parents feel uncomfortable leaving a very young baby in group care. If this applies to you, a childminder may offer a more home-like alternative. Read our [nursery vs childminder comparison](/blog/nursery-vs-childminder) for a balanced look at both options.

### 12 to 24 Months

This is one of the trickiest ages to start nursery because separation anxiety is often at its peak. Toddlers at this stage are deeply attached to their primary carers and may find the transition more distressing than a younger baby would.

**Advantages:** Children in this age range are becoming more mobile, curious, and social. A nursery provides stimulation, space, and interaction with other children that can support their rapid development.

**Considerations:** Be prepared for a longer settling-in period. Some toddlers take two to four weeks to feel comfortable. A good nursery will work with you to make this gradual and supportive. See our [settling-in tips](/blog/nursery-settling-in-tips) for practical advice.

### 2 to 3 Years

Many families start nursery at age two, partly because funded hours become available and partly because children at this age are developmentally ready for group experiences. Two-year-olds are learning to play alongside others, developing language rapidly, and becoming more independent.

**Advantages:** This is a natural transition point. Children are often ready for more social interaction and structured activities. The staff ratio of 1:4 means slightly larger groups, which many two-year-olds enjoy.

**Considerations:** Toilet training is often underway or approaching at this age. Ask potential nurseries about their toilet training approach and make sure it aligns with yours.

### 3 to 4 Years

Starting at three is very common. The universal 15-hour entitlement means every child has access to some free nursery time, and many settings offer a pre-school or kindergarten programme that prepares children for school.

**Advantages:** Children at three are generally confident enough to separate from parents, communicate their needs verbally, and engage with group activities. The transition to school a year or two later is smoother for children who have nursery experience.

**Considerations:** If your child has not been in any group setting before, they may initially find the noise and activity overwhelming. A gradual introduction still matters even at this age.

## Signs Your Child Is Ready for Nursery

Age alone does not determine readiness. Look for these signs that your child may be ready to benefit from nursery:

- **Curiosity about other children.** They watch other children with interest at the playground or in social settings.
- **Growing independence.** They can spend short periods engaged in activities without needing constant adult involvement.
- **Ability to communicate basic needs.** Whether through words, gestures, or pointing, they can let a carer know they are hungry, thirsty, tired, or need a nappy change.
- **Interest in new environments.** They enjoy exploring new places and are not overwhelmed by unfamiliar settings.
- **Separation tolerance.** They can manage short separations from you — even if they protest initially, they recover and engage with other people.

If your child does not show all of these signs, it does not mean they are not ready. Many children develop these skills through nursery attendance rather than before it.

## Signs It Might Be Worth Waiting

In some cases, it may make sense to delay starting nursery:

- Your child has significant separation anxiety that causes prolonged distress (not just a brief protest at drop-off)
- They are going through a major transition, such as the arrival of a new sibling, a house move, or a parental separation
- They have additional needs that require specialist support not yet in place
- You have flexible childcare alternatives and your child is thriving at home

There is no penalty for waiting. Children who start nursery at three develop just as well as those who start earlier, provided they have a stimulating home environment.

## How to Choose the Right Time of Year

If you have flexibility, consider the timing of your child's start date:

**September** is the most common start date and coincides with the new academic year at many nurseries. Your child will join alongside other new starters, which can make settling easier because everyone is in the same boat.

**January and April** are also common entry points. Mid-year starts can work well because staff have more capacity to focus on new children when the autumn cohort is already settled.

**Avoid starting during major routine changes.** If possible, do not start nursery at the same time as dropping a nap, night weaning, or potty training. Too many transitions at once can be overwhelming.

## Preparing Your Child

Whatever age you choose, a little preparation helps:

1. **Talk about nursery positively** without overdoing it. Mention it casually in conversation as an exciting thing that is coming up.
2. **Read books about starting nursery.** There are dozens of picture books on this theme. Your library will have several.
3. **Practise short separations.** Leave your child with a trusted friend or family member for increasing lengths of time.
4. **Visit the nursery together.** Most settings offer one or two pre-start visits. Let your child explore at their own pace while you are there.
5. **Establish a goodbye routine.** A consistent, brief goodbye — a hug, a wave, a special phrase — gives your child a predictable anchor point.

## The Settling-In Period

Almost every child finds the first few days or weeks of nursery challenging. This is normal and does not mean you have made the wrong decision. Most nurseries offer a structured settling-in period that starts with short visits and builds up gradually.

During this time:

- Drop-offs may involve tears. This is normal. Most children stop crying within minutes of the parent leaving.
- Your child may be more tired, clingy, or emotional at home. This is because nursery is hard work for a small person. Extra cuddles and early bedtimes help.
- Sleep and eating patterns may temporarily change. This usually settles within a few weeks.

If your child is still deeply distressed after three to four weeks of consistent attendance, speak to the nursery about adjusting the approach. For detailed guidance, read our [nursery settling-in tips](/blog/nursery-settling-in-tips).

## Finding the Right Nursery

Timing matters, but so does choosing the right setting. A warm, responsive nursery with experienced staff can make even a tricky transition smooth. A poorly managed one can make an easy-going child anxious.

## The Bottom Line

There is no perfect age to start nursery. Children are resilient and adaptable, and most settle happily given time, patience, and the right environment. Trust your knowledge of your own child, prepare thoughtfully, choose a nursery you feel confident about, and give the process the time it needs.

Whether your child starts at six months or three years, the experience of nursery — learning to be part of a group, forming new relationships, and exploring the world beyond home — is valuable at any age.
