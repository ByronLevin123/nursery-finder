# 15 Questions to Ask on a Nursery Visit

Visiting a nursery is the single most important step in choosing the right setting for your child. You can learn a lot from a website or an Ofsted report, but nothing replaces seeing a nursery in action and speaking to the people who will care for your child every day. The trouble is, nerves and information overload can mean you walk out without asking the things that really matter.

This is your ready-made checklist. Print it, save it on your phone, or memorise the key questions before you go. These 15 questions will give you a clear and honest picture of any nursery you visit.

## Before the Visit

Use the [NurseryMatch search tool](/search) to shortlist nurseries based on location, Ofsted rating, and distance. Read the latest inspection report for each nursery before you visit so you know what inspectors highlighted as strengths and areas for improvement. This gives you a foundation for your questions and helps you spot whether anything has changed since the last inspection.

## The 15 Questions

### 1. What Are Your Staff-to-Child Ratios?

The legal minimums in England are 1:3 for children under two, 1:4 for two-year-olds, and 1:8 for three to four-year-olds. The best nurseries often exceed these minimums. Ask what the actual ratios are during a typical day, not just the policy on paper.

**Why it matters:** Lower ratios mean more individual attention for your child, especially during the early months when they are settling in.

### 2. What Qualifications Do Your Staff Hold?

Ask about the proportion of staff with Level 3 qualifications or above, and whether the nursery has a qualified Early Years Teacher or Early Years Professional. Find out whether the nursery supports ongoing training and professional development.

**Why it matters:** Research consistently shows that staff qualifications are one of the strongest predictors of nursery quality.

### 3. How Do You Handle the Settling-In Period?

Every nursery should have a settling-in policy. Ask how they structure the first few weeks: do they offer short taster sessions? Can a parent stay? How do they help children who find the transition difficult?

**Why it matters:** A thoughtful settling-in process reduces stress for both you and your child. Read our [settling-in tips guide](/blog/nursery-settling-in-tips) for more on this.

### 4. What Is Your Staff Turnover Like?

This question sometimes catches nurseries off guard, but it is one of the most revealing things you can ask. High turnover means children constantly lose familiar carers and have to form new attachments.

**Why it matters:** Consistency of care is crucial for young children's emotional development. A nursery that retains its staff is usually doing many things right.

### 5. How Do You Communicate with Parents?

Ask about daily updates, parent apps, learning journals, and parent evenings. Find out how you will hear about your child's day, their developmental progress, and any concerns.

**Why it matters:** Good communication builds trust and helps you stay connected to your child's experience.

### 6. What Does a Typical Day Look Like?

Ask them to walk you through a normal day from drop-off to pick-up. Look for a balance of structured activities, free play, outdoor time, rest, meals, and snacks.

**Why it matters:** Children thrive on routine, but they also need variety and choice. A well-planned day supports both.

### 7. How Much Outdoor Time Do Children Get?

Children should be going outside every day, regardless of weather. Ask how often they go out, for how long, and what outdoor activities are available. If the nursery does not have its own garden, ask how they access outdoor space.

**Why it matters:** Outdoor play supports physical development, mental health, and a connection to nature that indoor activities cannot replicate.

### 8. What Is Your Approach to Behaviour Management?

Ask how staff handle conflict between children, tantrums, biting, and other challenging behaviours. The answer should focus on positive strategies like distraction, modelling, and emotional coaching, never punishment or isolation.

**Why it matters:** How a nursery manages behaviour tells you a lot about its culture and values.

### 9. What Meals and Snacks Do You Provide?

Find out what children eat and drink during the day. Are meals freshly prepared on site or delivered? Do they cater for allergies and dietary requirements? Is food included in the fees or charged separately?

**Why it matters:** Good nutrition supports learning and development. Mealtimes should also be social, relaxed, and enjoyable.

### 10. How Do You Support Children with Additional Needs?

Even if your child does not have identified additional needs, this question reveals how inclusive and adaptable the nursery is. Ask about their experience with speech and language delays, autism, physical disabilities, and other needs.

**Why it matters:** A nursery that supports all children well is generally a well-run nursery. Inclusive settings benefit every child.

### 11. What Is Your Safeguarding Policy?

Ask who the designated safeguarding lead is, how they handle concerns, and what training staff receive. The nursery should have clear policies displayed and staff should be confident discussing them.

**Why it matters:** Safeguarding is non-negotiable. Any reluctance to discuss it openly is a red flag.

### 12. Do You Offer Funded Places?

If your child is eligible for 15 or 30 funded hours, ask whether the nursery offers these and whether there are any conditions or additional charges. Some nurseries require funded children to attend a minimum number of paid hours alongside the free entitlement.

**Why it matters:** Funded hours can save you hundreds of pounds per month. Read our [free childcare hours guide](/blog/free-childcare-hours-guide) for eligibility details.

### 13. What Are Your Fees, and What Is Included?

Get a complete breakdown of costs, including any charges for meals, consumables, nappies, and late collection. Ask about deposit requirements, notice periods, and whether fees are charged during holidays when the nursery is closed.

**Why it matters:** Hidden costs can add up quickly. A transparent fee structure is a sign of a well-managed nursery.

### 14. How Do You Track and Share Children's Development?

Ask about learning journals, development reports, and the Early Years Foundation Stage (EYFS) framework. Find out how the nursery identifies and responds to developmental concerns, and when they involve parents in those conversations.

**Why it matters:** Early identification of developmental needs leads to earlier support, which makes a significant difference.

### 15. What Happens When My Child Is Unwell?

Ask about the illness policy: how long must a child stay away after vomiting or diarrhoea? How do they handle minor injuries? What is the procedure for administering prescribed medication?

**Why it matters:** Clear, sensible illness policies protect your child and every other child in the setting.

## Beyond the Questions: What to Observe

Your questions will give you factual answers, but your eyes and instincts are equally valuable. While you are at the nursery, notice the following:

- **Are children happy and engaged?** Look for smiles, laughter, and purposeful activity.
- **How do staff interact with children?** Are they warm, patient, and responsive? Do they get down to the children's level?
- **Is the environment clean and well-maintained?** Tidy does not mean sterile — a well-used nursery will have paint on the tables and mud on the wellies — but it should be hygienic and safe.
- **Is there a calm, welcoming atmosphere?** Every nursery has noisy moments, but the overall feeling should be one of warmth and security.
- **Do children have access to a range of resources?** Books, art materials, construction toys, role play areas, and sensory activities should all be visible and accessible.

## After the Visit

Take notes immediately after each visit while your impressions are fresh. Compare your shortlisted nurseries using the [NurseryMatch comparison tool](/search) to weigh up ratings, costs, and location alongside your personal observations.

Remember that no nursery is perfect, and the right choice for one family might not suit another. Focus on the things that matter most to you and your child, and trust your judgement. If something felt wrong during the visit, it probably was.
