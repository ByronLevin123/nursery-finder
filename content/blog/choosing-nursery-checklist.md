# The Ultimate Nursery Checklist: 25 Things to Look For

Choosing a nursery involves weighing up dozens of factors, and it is easy to forget important things in the excitement and anxiety of visits. This checklist covers everything you should consider, organised into clear categories. Use it as a guide during your research and as a prompt during visits.

Save this page, print it, or keep it on your phone. Tick off each item for every nursery you visit, and you will have a solid basis for comparison when it is time to decide.

## Before You Visit: Research Phase

### 1. Ofsted Rating

Check the nursery's current Ofsted grade. Outstanding and Good are the grades you are looking for, but read the full report rather than relying on the headline alone. Pay attention to the date of the last inspection — a rating from several years ago may not reflect the nursery today.

Use the [NurseryMatch search tool](/search) to filter nurseries by rating and read inspection reports directly from each nursery's profile page.

### 2. Inspection Date

An inspection more than four years old should prompt extra scrutiny. The nursery may still be excellent, but you have less recent evidence to go on. NurseryMatch flags nurseries with older inspections so you can spot them easily.

### 3. Enforcement Notices

Check whether the nursery has any active enforcement notices or conditions on its registration. These are serious and indicate that Ofsted has identified significant failings. NurseryMatch displays these prominently on affected profiles.

### 4. Location and Commute

A nursery that adds 30 minutes to your daily commute will quickly become a burden. Consider nurseries near your home, your workplace, or on your route between the two. Factor in traffic, parking, and public transport options.

### 5. Fees and Affordability

Get a clear picture of total costs before you visit. Ask about daily and weekly rates, what is included, and what is extra. Compare several nurseries in your area — fees can vary by 30% or more within the same postcode. Use our [nursery costs guide](/blog/nursery-costs-uk-2026) for average prices by region.

### 6. Funded Hours Availability

If your child qualifies for 15 or 30 funded hours, confirm that the nursery offers them and ask about any conditions. Some nurseries require funded children to take additional paid hours. Read our [free childcare hours guide](/blog/free-childcare-hours-guide) for full eligibility details.

### 7. Opening Hours

Check that the nursery's hours cover your work schedule with a comfortable margin. Most nurseries open from 7:30am to 6:00pm, but some close earlier. Ask about late pick-up policies and charges.

## During Your Visit: The Environment

### 8. Cleanliness and Hygiene

The nursery should be clean, hygienic, and well-maintained. This does not mean spotless — an active nursery will have paint on the tables and sand on the floor — but kitchens, bathrooms, and nappy-changing areas should be properly clean.

### 9. Safety

Look for secure entry systems, safety gates where appropriate, covered electrical outlets, and safe outdoor areas. Risk should be managed, not eliminated — children need some physical challenge — but obvious hazards should be addressed.

### 10. Outdoor Space

Outdoor play is essential for young children. Check whether the nursery has its own garden or outdoor area, how often children go outside, and what activities are available outdoors. If there is no on-site garden, ask how they access outdoor space — some nurseries use local parks daily.

### 11. Indoor Learning Environment

Look for a range of age-appropriate resources: books, art materials, construction toys, role play areas, sensory resources, and cosy spaces for rest. The environment should feel stimulating but not chaotic, with clear areas for different activities.

### 12. Display and Resources

Children's artwork on display is a good sign — it shows that children's efforts are valued. Resources should be accessible to children, not locked in cupboards. Books should be varied, in good condition, and reflect diverse families and experiences.

### 13. Noise Level and Atmosphere

Every nursery has noisy moments, but the overall atmosphere should feel warm, calm, and purposeful. Children should look engaged and content. If the environment feels tense, stressed, or chaotic during your visit, take that seriously.

## During Your Visit: The People

### 14. Staff Warmth and Responsiveness

This is arguably the most important thing on the entire list. Watch how staff interact with children. Are they warm, patient, and attentive? Do they get down to children's level? Do they respond promptly when a child is upset, needs help, or wants to share something?

### 15. Staff Qualifications

Ask about the proportion of staff with Level 3 qualifications or above, and whether the nursery employs a qualified Early Years Teacher. Higher qualifications correlate with higher quality provision.

### 16. Staff-to-Child Ratios

Confirm the actual ratios, not just the legal minimums. The best nurseries exceed the minimum requirements of 1:3 for under-twos, 1:4 for two-year-olds, and 1:8 for three to four-year-olds.

### 17. Staff Turnover

Ask directly about staff retention. High turnover is disruptive for children who need consistent, familiar carers. A nursery that keeps its staff is usually a nursery that treats them well.

### 18. The Manager

The manager sets the tone for the entire nursery. They should be knowledgeable, approachable, and visibly involved in the daily life of the setting. If the manager is distant or dismissive during your visit, consider what that says about the culture.

## During Your Visit: Policies and Practice

### 19. Settling-In Process

Ask how the nursery handles new children. There should be a structured settling-in period with short initial visits that gradually increase. The pace should be guided by the child's needs. For more on this, read our [settling-in tips](/blog/nursery-settling-in-tips).

### 20. Communication with Parents

Find out how the nursery keeps you informed about your child's day. Daily updates (via an app, diary, or verbal handover), regular parent evenings, and accessible learning journals are all good signs. Read our [questions to ask on a nursery visit](/blog/questions-to-ask-nursery-visit) for more on this topic.

### 21. Behaviour Management

Ask how staff handle challenging behaviour. The approach should be positive, consistent, and age-appropriate — using distraction, modelling, and emotional coaching rather than punishment or isolation.

### 22. Meals and Nutrition

Find out what meals and snacks are provided, whether they are prepared on site, and how allergies and dietary needs are managed. Mealtimes should be social, relaxed experiences, not rushed or stressful.

### 23. Safeguarding

Ask who the designated safeguarding lead is and what training staff receive. Safeguarding policies should be clearly displayed, and staff should be confident discussing the procedures. Any reluctance to engage on this topic is a red flag.

### 24. Inclusion and Additional Needs

Ask how the nursery supports children with additional needs, including speech and language delays, physical disabilities, and behavioural needs. An inclusive nursery benefits all children, not just those with identified needs.

### 25. Your Gut Feeling

After everything on this list, trust your instincts. Did the nursery feel right? Could you picture your child happy there? Did the staff seem genuinely caring? Your gut feeling synthesises dozens of small observations that are hard to articulate individually.

If something felt off during the visit, do not ignore it. Equally, if a nursery ticked most boxes and you felt a genuine warmth, that matters too.

## After Your Visits: Making the Decision

Once you have visited your shortlisted nurseries, compare them systematically:

1. **Review your notes.** Go through this checklist for each nursery and note where they were strong and where they were weaker.

2. **Compare side by side.** Use the [NurseryMatch comparison tool](/search) to compare Ofsted ratings, fees, distance, and key features for your shortlisted nurseries.

3. **Weigh your priorities.** No nursery will score perfectly on all 25 items. Decide which factors matter most to your family. For some, location is paramount. For others, staff quality outweighs everything else. For many, affordability is the deciding factor.

4. **Talk it through.** Discuss your impressions with your partner, family, or friends. Sometimes articulating your thoughts to someone else helps clarify your decision.

5. **Make a decision and commit.** Once you have done your research and visited your options, make a decision and commit to it. You can always revisit the decision later if your circumstances change.

## A Final Note

Choosing a nursery is a significant decision, but it is not an irreversible one. If the nursery you choose turns out not to be right for your family, you can move. Children are adaptable, and a well-managed change is far better than persisting with a setting that is not working.

The fact that you are reading a 25-point checklist means you are taking this decision seriously, and that is the most important thing of all. Your child is lucky to have a parent who cares this much about getting it right.
