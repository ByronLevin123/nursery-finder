# What Happens During an Ofsted Nursery Inspection?

Every nursery in England is inspected by Ofsted — the Office for Standards in Education, Children's Services and Skills. These inspections determine the rating that appears alongside every nursery's name, and they play a major role in how parents choose childcare. But what actually happens during an inspection? What do inspectors look for, and how should you interpret the results?

This guide takes you behind the scenes of the Ofsted inspection process, explains what each rating means, and shows you how to use inspection reports as a practical tool when choosing a nursery.

## How Often Are Nurseries Inspected?

The frequency of inspections depends on the nursery's previous rating:

- **Outstanding:** Typically not routinely inspected again unless concerns arise, though Ofsted has been increasing the frequency of re-inspections for Outstanding settings in recent years. Some Outstanding nurseries may go several years without inspection.
- **Good:** Inspected approximately every four years, though Ofsted can inspect sooner if concerns are raised.
- **Requires Improvement:** Re-inspected within 12 to 18 months to check whether the nursery has addressed the issues identified.
- **Inadequate:** Subject to more frequent monitoring and re-inspection, often within 6 to 12 months. In serious cases, enforcement action may be taken.

New nurseries are typically inspected within 30 months of registration.

## What Happens Before the Inspection

Nurseries usually receive a phone call from Ofsted on the morning of the inspection or the afternoon before. This short notice is deliberate — it gives the nursery just enough time to ensure the manager is present, but not enough time to artificially prepare.

In some cases, inspections are unannounced. This is more common when Ofsted has received complaints or concerns about a setting.

Before arriving, the inspector reviews the nursery's previous inspection report, any complaints or concerns received since the last inspection, and the nursery's self-evaluation (if one has been submitted).

## What Happens During the Inspection

A typical nursery inspection lasts around four to five hours, usually taking place in a single day. Here is what the inspector does:

### Observing Practice

The inspector spends a significant portion of the visit observing staff interacting with children. They watch how practitioners respond to children's needs, how activities are planned and delivered, and how the learning environment is set up.

Key things inspectors look for:

- **Quality of interactions.** Are staff having meaningful conversations with children? Are they asking open-ended questions that extend thinking?
- **Child engagement.** Are children engaged, curious, and making progress? Are they being challenged appropriately for their age and stage?
- **Emotional wellbeing.** Do children appear happy, settled, and secure? How do staff respond when a child is distressed?
- **Safeguarding in action.** Are children supervised appropriately? Is the environment safe? Do staff follow hygiene and safety procedures?

### Joint Observations

The inspector often conducts a "joint observation" with the nursery manager. They watch an activity together and then discuss what they saw: what was effective, what could be improved, and how the manager monitors quality across the setting.

This tells the inspector a lot about the manager's understanding of good practice and their ability to lead improvement.

### Reviewing Documentation

Inspectors review key documents including:

- Safeguarding policies and records
- Staff qualification records and DBS checks
- Accident and incident records
- Children's learning journals and development records
- Risk assessments
- The nursery's self-evaluation and improvement plan

### Talking to Staff

The inspector speaks to several members of staff at different levels. They ask about safeguarding procedures, how they plan activities for individual children, how they identify and respond to concerns about development, and how they work with parents.

Staff who are knowledgeable, confident, and reflective about their practice make a positive impression. Those who cannot articulate their approach or seem unsure about key policies raise concerns.

### Talking to Parents

Inspectors also gather the views of parents. They may speak to parents at drop-off or pick-up, or review written feedback. Ofsted also considers responses submitted to its Parent View online survey before and during the inspection.

### Speaking with the Manager and Registered Person

The inspector has a detailed conversation with the nursery manager about leadership, quality of provision, staff development, and plans for improvement. They also verify that the registered person (the individual or organisation legally responsible for the nursery) is meeting their statutory obligations.

## What Do the Four Ratings Mean?

### Outstanding

The nursery provides exceptional care and education. Children make rapid progress, staff practice is exemplary, leadership is highly effective, and safeguarding is rigorous. Outstanding nurseries go well beyond meeting minimum requirements.

**What to know as a parent:** Outstanding is the highest grade, but it is not the only mark of a good nursery. Some Outstanding nurseries have not been re-inspected for years, so the rating may reflect a past, not present, standard.

### Good

The nursery meets a high standard across all areas. Children are well cared for, make good progress, staff are effective, and leadership ensures consistent quality. The vast majority of nurseries in England are rated Good.

**What to know as a parent:** A Good rating is genuinely good. Do not feel that your child is missing out if the nursery is not Outstanding. Read the report for specifics about what works well and what could improve.

### Requires Improvement

The nursery is not yet Good. There are weaknesses in one or more areas that need to be addressed. The nursery will be re-inspected within 12 to 18 months.

**What to know as a parent:** This rating does not necessarily mean the nursery is unsafe. It often reflects issues like inconsistent planning, limited staff development, or aspects of leadership that need strengthening. Read the report carefully to understand the specific concerns. If the nursery has recently changed management or made visible improvements, it may be on an upward trajectory.

### Inadequate

The nursery has serious weaknesses. Children's safety, wellbeing, or learning may be at risk. The nursery must make urgent improvements and will be closely monitored.

**What to know as a parent:** An Inadequate rating is a serious concern. Ofsted may take enforcement action, including suspending or cancelling the nursery's registration. If your child attends an Inadequate-rated nursery, read the report urgently and consider your options.

## How to Read an Ofsted Report

Every Ofsted report follows a standard structure. Here is how to get the most from it:

1. **Start with the overall judgement,** but do not stop there. The headline grade is useful, but the detail matters more.

2. **Read the "What the setting needs to do to improve" section.** This tells you the specific weaknesses the inspector identified. Check whether these are minor (like improving outdoor provision) or significant (like safeguarding concerns).

3. **Look at the date.** An Outstanding report from six years ago tells you less than a Good report from last year. On NurseryMatch, nurseries with inspections older than four years display a [stale grade banner](/blog/understanding-ofsted-ratings) to flag this.

4. **Check for enforcement notices.** If the report mentions enforcement action or conditions on registration, this is a red flag. NurseryMatch displays enforcement banners on affected nursery profiles.

5. **Read between the lines.** Phrases like "most children" (rather than "all children") and "generally" (rather than "consistently") signal that practice is not yet uniformly strong.

## What If You Disagree with an Inspection?

If you are a parent and you believe an inspection report does not accurately reflect the nursery you know, you can submit feedback to Ofsted. Nurseries themselves can submit a formal complaint if they believe the inspection was unfair.

Equally, if your experience of a nursery is worse than the report suggests — for example, if the nursery received a Good rating but you have witnessed poor practice — you can raise concerns with Ofsted directly through their complaints process.

## The Bigger Picture

Ofsted inspections are imperfect. A single-day visit cannot capture everything about a nursery. Ratings can be influenced by the inspector, the day, and the specific group of children present. Some excellent nurseries receive Good rather than Outstanding, and some average nurseries have legacy Outstanding ratings.

Use Ofsted reports as one important piece of the puzzle, alongside parent reviews, personal visits, and your own instincts. The combination of data and direct experience gives you the best chance of finding a nursery where your child will be safe, happy, and thriving.
