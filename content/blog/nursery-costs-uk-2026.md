# How Much Does Nursery Cost in the UK in 2026?

Childcare is one of the largest household expenses for families with young children in the UK. Understanding what nursery costs look like in 2026 — and knowing how to reduce them — can make a real difference to your family budget. This guide covers average fees by region, what is included in the price, and every option available to bring costs down.

## The National Picture

The average cost of a full-time nursery place (50 hours per week) for a child under two in England is approximately £1,190 per month in 2026. For children aged two and above, the average drops to around £1,080 per month, reflecting slightly lower staff-to-child ratio requirements.

These are averages, and your actual costs will depend heavily on where you live, the type of setting you choose, and how many hours you need.

## What Is Included in Nursery Fees?

Most nursery fees cover the core childcare and early education provision. However, what counts as extra varies between settings:

**Usually included:** Nappies and wipes (at many nurseries), snacks, structured activities, and access to all on-site resources.

**Sometimes included:** Meals. Some nurseries include all meals in the fee, while others charge a daily meal supplement of £3 to £8. Always ask.

**Usually extra:** Uniform or branded clothing, consumables fees (a termly charge of £20 to £50 at some settings), late collection penalties (typically £10 to £15 per 15-minute block), and special outings or trips.

When comparing nursery costs, make sure you are comparing like for like. A nursery that appears cheaper may cost more once meal supplements and consumables are added.

## Free Childcare Hours: Your Biggest Cost Saver

The single most effective way to reduce nursery costs is to make full use of your funded entitlement.

### 15 Hours Universal Entitlement

All three and four-year-olds in England are entitled to 15 hours of free childcare per week (570 hours per year), regardless of household income. This applies from the term after your child turns three.

### 30 Hours Extended Entitlement

Working parents of three and four-year-olds may qualify for 30 hours of free childcare per week. Both parents (or a single parent) must earn at least the equivalent of 16 hours per week at the National Minimum Wage and no more than £100,000 per year individually.

### Working Parent Entitlement for Under-Threes

From 2024, the government expanded funded childcare to younger children. In 2026, eligible working parents can access 15 hours of funded care from when their child is nine months old, expanding to 30 hours from age two. Check your eligibility on the [GOV.UK Childcare Choices website](https://www.childcarechoices.gov.uk).

For a complete breakdown of all funded entitlements, read our [free childcare hours guide](/blog/free-childcare-hours-guide).

### How Much Do Funded Hours Save?

At the national average hourly rate, 30 funded hours per week saves you roughly £700 to £900 per month. Even the 15-hour universal entitlement saves £350 to £450 per month. These savings are substantial and make a material difference to most family budgets.

## Tax-Free Childcare

If you are not using Tax Credits or Universal Credit, you may be eligible for Tax-Free Childcare. The government tops up your childcare payments by 20p for every 80p you pay, up to a maximum of £2,000 per child per year (or £4,000 for disabled children).

You pay into an online account, the government adds the top-up, and you use the account to pay your nursery directly. It is straightforward to set up and can save you up to £167 per month.

## Childcare Vouchers and Salary Sacrifice

The Childcare Vouchers scheme closed to new entrants in October 2018, but if you were already registered, you can continue to use it. Basic-rate taxpayers save up to £933 per year; higher-rate taxpayers save up to £624 per year.

Some employers offer salary sacrifice childcare schemes that work similarly. Check with your HR department.

## Universal Credit Childcare Element

If you receive Universal Credit, you can claim back up to 85% of your childcare costs, up to £1,014.63 per month for one child or £1,739.37 for two or more children. You pay the nursery first and then claim the costs back through your Universal Credit journal.

## Employer-Supported Childcare

Some employers offer nursery places, childcare subsidies, or emergency backup care as part of their benefits package. These are more common at larger employers and in the public sector. It is always worth asking your employer what support is available.

## Practical Tips to Reduce Nursery Costs

### 1. Use All Your Funded Hours

This sounds obvious, but many parents do not claim their full entitlement. Make sure you apply before the deadline for each term.

### 2. Consider Part-Time Options

If your work allows flexibility, reducing from five days to three or four can save hundreds per month. Some nurseries offer discounted rates for part-time attendance.

### 3. Compare Nurseries Carefully

Fees can vary by 30% or more between nurseries in the same postcode. Use the [NurseryMatch comparison tool](/search) to compare costs alongside quality ratings. The cheapest option is not always the best value, but neither is the most expensive always the highest quality.

### 4. Stretch Your Hours

Instead of using 30 funded hours across three full days, some nurseries let you spread them across five shorter days or use them flexibly across the week. This can reduce the number of additional paid hours you need.

### 5. Check for Sibling Discounts

Many nurseries offer 5% to 15% off fees for a second child attending at the same time. Always ask, even if it is not advertised.

### 6. Negotiate Term-Time Only

If you do not need year-round care, term-time only places are typically 15% to 20% cheaper than all-year-round places.

### 7. Combine Care Types

Some families use a combination of nursery and grandparent care, or nursery and childminder, to cover the week more affordably. Read our [nursery vs childminder comparison](/blog/nursery-vs-childminder) for more on blending care types.

## Is It Worth Paying More for a Better Nursery?

Quality matters, but cost and quality do not always correlate directly. Some of the highest-rated nurseries in the country are community or local authority settings with modest fees. Conversely, some expensive private nurseries receive average Ofsted grades.

Focus on what you are getting for the money: qualified staff, a stimulating environment, strong safeguarding, and happy children. Use NurseryMatch to compare [Ofsted ratings](/blog/nursery-inspection-what-to-expect) alongside costs so you can make an informed decision.

## Planning Your Nursery Budget

When budgeting for nursery, account for the full picture:

- **Base fees** for the days and hours you need
- **Minus funded hours** (check eligibility carefully)
- **Minus Tax-Free Childcare top-up** (if eligible)
- **Plus extras** like meals, consumables, and uniform
- **Plus contingency** for late pick-up fees and holiday cover

This gives you a realistic monthly figure. For most families, nursery costs are temporary — typically three to four years — and the investment in your child's early development is significant. But there is no reason to pay more than you need to, so take the time to explore every option available to you.
