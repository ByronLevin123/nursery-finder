# Nursery Settling In: 10 Tips for a Smooth Transition

Starting nursery is a milestone for your child and for you. Whether your little one is six months or three years old, the transition from home to a group setting involves adjusting to new people, new routines, and time apart. Some children take to nursery immediately. Others need weeks to feel comfortable. Both responses are completely normal.

These ten tips are drawn from early years professionals and the real experiences of parents. They will help you prepare for the settling-in period and support your child through it.

## What Is the Settling-In Period?

Most nurseries offer a structured settling-in process, typically lasting one to two weeks. This usually starts with short visits of an hour or two, with a parent present, and gradually increases to longer sessions where the child stays alone. The pace should be led by your child's needs, not a rigid timetable.

If you are still choosing a nursery, ask about their settling-in policy during your visit. It is one of the [key questions to ask](/blog/questions-to-ask-nursery-visit) and reveals a lot about how child-centred the setting is.

## The 10 Tips

### 1. Start Practising Separations Early

In the weeks before nursery starts, practise short separations. Leave your child with a trusted friend or family member for an hour, then two hours, then longer. This builds their confidence that you always come back.

Do not make these trial separations a big deal. Keep your goodbye brief and cheerful, and be upbeat when you return. Your child is learning that separation is temporary and safe.

### 2. Talk About Nursery Calmly and Positively

Mention nursery in casual conversation before it starts: "Soon you are going to go to nursery where you can play with other children." Read picture books about starting nursery — there are dozens of good ones, and your local library will stock several.

Avoid over-selling it ("It is going to be the BEST day EVER!") because this sets up expectations that the first confusing morning cannot meet. A calm, matter-of-fact tone is more reassuring than forced excitement.

### 3. Establish a Goodbye Routine

Create a short, consistent goodbye ritual: a hug, a kiss on the forehead, a wave at the window, a special phrase like "See you after snack time." Use the same routine every single day.

Children find predictability comforting. A consistent goodbye tells them what to expect and signals that the separation follows a familiar pattern. Keep it brief — prolonged goodbyes increase anxiety for everyone.

### 4. Never Sneak Away

It is tempting to slip out when your child is distracted, but this damages trust. Your child needs to see you leave and know that you said goodbye properly. If they cry at the door, that is painful to witness, but it is honest. Most children stop crying within minutes once the parent has left.

Ask the nursery to send you a message or photo once your child has settled. Knowing they recovered quickly makes the next drop-off easier for you.

### 5. Bring a Comfort Object

A familiar toy, blanket, or even a piece of clothing that smells like home can be enormously comforting. Check with the nursery that comfort objects are welcome (most are) and label it clearly with your child's name.

Some parents give their child a small family photo to keep in their pocket or tray. This provides a tangible connection to home during the day.

### 6. Keep Mornings Calm and Predictable

Rushed, chaotic mornings increase everyone's stress. Prepare bags, clothes, and food the night before so that the morning routine is calm and unhurried. Leave enough time to arrive without rushing so you can do a proper handover.

If your child senses that you are stressed or anxious, they will pick up on it. Your calm demeanour signals that everything is fine, even if you are feeling emotional inside.

### 7. Trust the Nursery Staff

Early years practitioners have settled hundreds of children. They know the strategies that work: distraction, comfort, favourite activities at the door, and gentle reassurance. Let them do their job. If you hover or keep coming back to check, it sends your child the message that there is something to worry about.

That said, communication is essential. Talk to your child's key worker after each session and share anything that might help — routines at home, favourite songs, comfort triggers. A good nursery will use this information to help your child feel secure. If you want to assess how well a nursery communicates, review our [questions to ask on a visit](/blog/questions-to-ask-nursery-visit).

### 8. Be Consistent with Attendance

During the settling-in period, try to maintain a consistent schedule. If your child attends three days a week, keep those days the same. Gaps of several days between sessions can reset the settling process, as your child has to readjust each time.

If possible, avoid taking a holiday in the first few weeks of nursery. The momentum of regular attendance helps children build familiarity and routine.

### 9. Expect Some Behavioural Changes at Home

It is very common for children to be more tired, clingy, or emotionally volatile during the first few weeks of nursery. They may sleep more, eat differently, or regress in areas where they were previously independent (for example, wanting to be carried more or reverting to a bottle).

This is not a sign that nursery is harming them. It is a sign that they are processing a big change. Respond with extra warmth and patience, and give them plenty of downtime at home. Early bedtimes help enormously.

### 10. Give It Time

Most children settle within two to four weeks of consistent attendance. Some take longer, particularly if they are starting during a developmental phase like separation anxiety (common between 8 and 18 months) or during another life change.

If your child is still deeply distressed after four weeks — not just a brief cry at drop-off, but prolonged distress that does not resolve once you leave — talk to the nursery about whether the approach needs adjusting. In rare cases, a different settling schedule, a change of key worker, or even a different setting may be the answer.

## What About You?

Parents often underestimate how emotional the settling-in period is for them. It is entirely normal to feel guilty, anxious, or tearful, even if you are confident in your choice of nursery. Talk to other parents who have been through it, lean on your partner or friends, and be kind to yourself.

If your child is your first, remember that this transition is as new for you as it is for them. It gets easier.

## Signs That Settling Is Going Well

Look for these positive indicators:

- Your child is happy to see their key worker
- They talk about nursery friends or activities at home
- Drop-off tears are brief and they recover quickly
- They are engaged and content when you collect them
- They are eating and sleeping normally (after the initial adjustment)

## When to Be Concerned

Seek a conversation with the nursery manager if:

- Your child is still deeply distressed after four or more weeks
- They show signs of anxiety at home that were not present before (persistent nightmares, refusing food, significant behavioural regression)
- The nursery is not communicating with you about how your child is doing
- Your instincts tell you something is not right

Your instincts matter. If something feels wrong, investigate it rather than dismissing your concerns.

## Choosing the Right Nursery Makes Settling Easier

The single biggest factor in a smooth transition is choosing a nursery that is warm, responsive, and well-managed. A good nursery will work with you and your child as a team, adjusting the pace of settling to meet your child's individual needs.
